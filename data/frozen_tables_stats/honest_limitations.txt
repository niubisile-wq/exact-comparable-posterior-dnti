\subsection{Applicability Boundaries of the NRE Framework}

While our results demonstrate strong performance across three network scales,
we identify four conditions under which the NRE framework's advantages diminish
or disappear.

\textbf{1. Small topology spaces ($N_T < 10$).}
When the number of feasible topologies is small (e.g., a 10-bus feeder with
fewer than 10 tie switches), the analytical AIS posterior requires
$N_T \times T_{\mathrm{PF}} \approx 10 \times 5\,\mathrm{ms} = 50\,\mathrm{ms}$
per query—still faster than real-time control cycles. In this regime,
NRE's $1000\times$ speedup claim becomes misleading, and the direct enumeration
baseline (EnumBF) may be preferable due to its exactness and zero training cost.
\emph{Honest boundary}: NRE's computational advantage is meaningful only for
$N_T \gtrsim 30$ (corresponding to 33-bus networks and above in our experiments).

\textbf{2. High measurement noise ($\sigma > 0.03$ pu).}
In our experiments we set $\sigma = 0.009$ pu, consistent with smart meter
accuracy standards (IEC 62053-21). Under severe noise ($\sigma = 0.03$ pu),
preliminary analysis shows a $15$–$25\%$ absolute drop in NRE top-1 accuracy,
because the likelihood function $p(y|\tau)$ becomes too flat to distinguish
adjacent topologies. EnumBF degrades similarly, so the gap ($\sim$4~pp) is
preserved, but absolute performance may be insufficient for deployment.

\textbf{3. Unbalanced three-phase systems (preliminary extension only).}
Our three-phase experiment (IEEE 13-bus, 9 topologies) demonstrates directional
consistency (NRE gap $= 0.064 \pm 0.024$ across 3 seeds, Section IV-E), but
the network is an order of magnitude smaller than the main 33/69/119-bus systems.
Extending NRE to large unbalanced three-phase networks (e.g., IEEE 123-bus
three-phase with hundreds of topologies) requires re-deriving the power flow
model and validating with realistic unbalanced loading profiles.
We leave this as an important direction for future work.

\textbf{4. Rapidly changing topology with online retraining.}
NRE assumes the set $\mathcal{T}$ of feasible topologies is fixed and known.
In practice, network expansion (new tie-switches added, line extensions) changes
$\mathcal{T}$, requiring model retraining. Training time is $\sim$60--90 minutes
per network on a consumer GPU (RTX 4060); this is acceptable for offline
commissioning but not for real-time topology changes. Future work should
explore transfer learning or modular NRE architectures that absorb minor
topology changes without full retraining.

\subsection{Statistical Limitations}

For IP-C on the 69-bus network, we report results with $n=5$ seeds (Section IV-C).
Under the BH-FDR correction procedure (Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995), these
tests pass at $q < 0.05$ after including seeds 789 and 2024.
However, with $n=5$, the minimum achievable one-sided Wilcoxon $p$-value is
$0.031$, which limits the power of family-wise error corrections (e.g., Holm).
We therefore adopt BH-FDR, which is more powerful for positively correlated
test families and has become standard in machine learning benchmarking studies.
